03 · Applied-risk econometrics the spine

co-primary · ~6 h/wk · the most important lane.

What this lane is. The actual subject of the career and the research: the statistics of financial risk — modelling how volatile a market is, how often extreme losses happen, and how large they can get. Two core texts carry it: Tsay, Analysis of Financial Time Series (the standard applied graduate text) and McNeil–Frey–Embrechts, Quantitative Risk Management (“QRM”) (the standard risk text).

The models to master. GARCH / GAS — volatility that clusters and changes over time. EVT (extreme-value theory) — the statistics of rare, extreme losses. VaR / ES (Value-at-Risk and Expected Shortfall) — the two headline loss numbers every bank must report and defend.

The differentiator. Not running a package and reading its output. For each model: derive the equations by hand, code the estimator from scratch, and check the numbers against an established package to a tight tolerance. That is the gap between someone who uses risk models and someone who can build and validate them — which is exactly what the PhD and the model-validation career hire for. This is the methods; the regulation (Basel / IRB / IFRS9) is learned on the job.

Each season below lists the ongoing work (where the ~6 h/week actually go) and the goalpost it ends on (the checkpoint). The reading/coding items fill a whole season — they are not one-week tasks.
Summer 2026 · now
Work through Tsay Chapters 1–2 (financial returns, their distributions, basic linear time series) with a keyboard — retype and run every R example yourself inside a proper project, not read passively. At ~6 h/week this is roughly the whole summer; the aim is fluency with the tools, not speed.
→ Goalpost: Chapters 1–2 fully reproduced in a version-controlled project — every example runs.
Autumn 2026
Tsay Chapters 1–3 (adding conditional heteroskedasticity — volatility that clusters) + QRM Chapter 2. Focus on understanding a GARCH model end to end: what it assumes, how it is estimated, what it is for.
→ Goalpost: you can estimate a GARCH model and explain precisely what it does.
Winter–Spring 2027 exam freeze — pace drops
Tsay Ch 3 (volatility) + Ch 7 (extreme-value theory); QRM Ch 3–5, 9 (VaR/ES, EVT, backtesting). Concepts only — the UoL exams take priority, so this lane slows but does not stop.
→ Goalpost: EVT and the VaR/ES risk measures understood conceptually.
Summer 2027
Consolidate QRM and begin deriving the estimators by hand — writing out the likelihood and update equations for GARCH and EVT yourself.
→ Goalpost: the core estimators derived on paper, ready to code.
Autumn 2027
Hand-code GARCH / GAS / EVT estimators from those derivations and run your first full reproductions of published results. This is the season the domain turns into an output.
→ ★ GOALPOST: a rung-1 VaR/ES backtest — your own code, validated against a package, producing a correct, reproducible risk-model backtest at a standard a risk desk would accept.
Winter–Spring 2028 finals — spine slips last
Build the PI-indexed reproduction: reproduce, and add a small extension to, a specific target professor’s own paper, from its equations. Under exam pressure this lane is protected the longest.
→ ★ GOALPOST: a drafted flagship artifact — a careful reproduction + a small extension of a target PI’s paper.
Summer 2028
Consolidate; hand the artifact to lane 01 for final polishing.
→ Goalpost: implement-then-research level reached; the artifact is ready.
End state (mid-2028): you can derive, implement, and numerically validate GARCH/GAS/EVT/VaR-ES from equations — and you have used that skill to build a research artifact. This feeds the MSc thesis, the professor cold-open, and the Swiss-career proof.
