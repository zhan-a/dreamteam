Roadmap — your multi-year map

The whole arc on one page: where you are now (Jun 2026) → a funded PhD. Read top-to-bottom when you lose the thread. Day-to-day to-dos live on the Tasks tab.

1. Where you're going

A funded research PhD → a high-comp job in a strong economy. Comp is the goal; the PhD is the vehicle, and PR is just what lets you take the job (US / EU / Canada all fine).

Which PhD, ranked: US top-20 ≥ strong UK/Swiss (Oxbridge · ETH/EPFL) ≥ {US top-40 ≈ Canadian}. Germany is only a last-line "never-stranded" floor. Apex US top-5 is a free swing, not the target — you won't burn years chasing it once a top-20 offer is in hand.

How you get in: apply direct + wide to US PhDs, and in parallel run a funded Canadian thesis-MSc as the bridge (Europe/Canada want a master's first, so it's infrastructure — and it feeds US/UK/Swiss/Canadian PhDs). Take the best funded offer when it comes. Most likely outcome: a funded sub-apex PhD — which already gets you the job and a place to stay.

5. The two gates that decide it

Everything above serves two things: (1) the MATH 447 A-grade (the real-analysis transcript line, Jun 2027) and (2) one calibrated research letter — so start the Lytkin/Mynbaev supervised project now, because it has to be mature by the Dec-2027 applications. Publications are not required. Protect these two and the rest is just execution.

6. When to stop

Accept and stop applying the moment a funded PhD you'd be happy in lands — US top-20, a strong UK/Swiss programme, or a good Canadian one. Apex top-5 is a bonus if it happens, not something to re-roll years for. A real offer in hand beats another cycle of waiting.

Self-study so it actually counts

Self-study only captures most of a real course's value if you enforce these — otherwise it's passive consumption that feels like learning but isn't.

RuleWhat it means
No premature consultationEccles/Abbott/Axler exercise: 45 min of real attempt before looking anything up. Pugh / MATH 447: ~2 hours across sittings. A Phase-0 paper concept: 30 min self-derivation before reading theirs.
Cold reproductionAfter each chapter, close the book and re-prove the main theorems on a blank page — next day, a week later, a month later. Keep a "proofs I can write cold" list.
Partial-step onlyWhen you do look: read only the first step you were missing, then close it and try again. Never import a finished proof.
Write something dailyDid you write at least one proof from a blank page today? Three "no" days in a row = you've slipped back into consumption.
Engineering ≠ conceptUse Claude / Stack Overflow freely for syntax and library friction. The discipline applies only to conceptual understanding.
Make it legibleFor Axler: a public GitHub repo with chapter + proof writeups (or a Stack Exchange profile). It replaces the missing transcript line in your SOP.

Phase 0 — reproduce it honestly

The one rule that makes the artifact credible: build your own implementation from the paper before you ever open the authors' repo (jjcherian/conditional-conformal). Your cold version must be git-committed before any commit that references theirs — that timestamp is what proves you built it, not copied it.
StepWhatRef repo?
Read (3 passes)Skim → careful + figures → line-by-line math; re-derive the marginal coverage theorem (A–B App. D) and state GCC Thm 1 (its conditional generalization). Gate: the closed-paper paper-sheet.md one-pager from memory.❌
Spec, then code coldPseudocode from the paper, your own directory layout; implement the 3 core functions (setup_cvx_problem · predict · estimate_coverage) with numpy/scipy/cvxpy/sklearn only. Gate: your version runs end-to-end.❌
Sanity runsOverfit one tiny batch; predict each plot's shape before running; reproduce the group-coverage figure within ±0.02.❌
ReconcileNow open their repo. Diff outputs on the same seed; log every >0.02 gap with its cause. This log is the cold-email artifact.✅
Extend + reportTwo pre-registered extensions; write it up in reproducibility-report format.✅

Two hard go/no-gos: (1) can't reproduce group coverage without peeking by the end of Week 2 → stop, drill Koenker Ch.3 + Boyd Ch.5 for 1–2 weeks, then resume — do not copy-paste through it. (2) Baseline doesn't match within ±0.02 by Week 4 → cut the extensions and fix the reproduction first. Realistic time-to-ship is ~8–10 weeks, not 6; slipping a couple of weeks is normal. Traps: reading code before writing your own · copy-paste-with-renames · "the notebook runs, so I get it" · skipping the proofs · extending before the baseline matches. The defense for all of them: write/derive before you read/run.
